▲ Northern Ireland — key difference from England & Wales
NI does not have a competent person self-certification scheme. All notifiable electrical work must be submitted to your District Council Building Control (form BC1) before or within 3 days of starting. A qualified electrician must issue an EIC, and Building Control issues the final completion certificate (BC5). This is required for property conveyancing.

⚠ Cable colour note — post-2006 harmonised colours
Since April 2006, all new wiring uses harmonised colours: Live = Brown, Neutral = Blue, Earth = Green/Yellow. Pre-2006 wiring uses Red (live) and Black (neutral). Intermediate (3-switch) circuit
Three or more switches independently control one light. The middle switch is a 4-terminal intermediate type that crosses or passes the traveller wires.
1.0mm² 3-core & earthCorridors with 3+ doors

▲ how intermediate switch works
The intermediate switch has 4 terminals. In one position it passes both traveller wires straight through. When toggled, it crosses them — swapping L1 and L2. This means the downstream 2-way switch's position is effectively reversed without it moving. Any switch in the chain can toggle the lamp. Add as many intermediates as needed for long corridors.
13A Ring Final Circuit
Sockets loop from the CU and back forming a ring — halving effective resistance and allowing 13A at any point. The most common circuit in NI homes.
2.5mm² T&E32A RCBONI: one ring per floor · max 100m²
▲ NI requirement — RCBO preferred
Max floor area per ring: 100m² (BS 7671). NI Building Control prefer one ring per floor on full rewires. All sockets must have 30mA RCD protection — use an RCBO per circuit. Spurs: maximum one non-fused spur (single/twin socket) per socket on ring, OR one fused spur via 13A FCU. You cannot spur from a spur.
⚡ confirming the ring
Test the ring by: (1) connecting line conductors end-to-end and measuring R1 — must be consistent halfway along the ring at each socket. (2) Repeat for neutral and CPC. An open ring or crossed connection will show an unusually high reading at some sockets. NI Building Control require ring continuity test results on the EIC.

▲ diversity calculation — BS 7671 Appendix 15
Don't size cable to the full rated current — use diversity: take the first 10A fully, then add 30% of the remaining current, plus 5A if the CCU includes a 13A socket. Example: 10kW cooker = 43A rated → 10 + (33 × 0.3) + 5 = 24.9A design current → 6mm² cable + 32A or 40A RCBO is correct. Range cookers over 14kW need 10mm².

Since BS 7671:2018+A2:2022, supplementary bonding in bathrooms is not required if ALL circuits in the bathroom are protected by a 30mA RCD AND main bonding is satisfactory. Outdoor socket
External sockets require RCD protection, appropriate IP rating, and must be on a dedicated circuit or protected spur. Cable must follow safe routes.

Cables buried externally must be: armoured (SWA) at any depth, or MICC (mineral insulated), or in earthed conduit buried to 500mm minimum depth. Loop at ceiling rose
The traditional UK lighting method — the supply loops through each ceiling rose in turn, with a switch cable dropping down to the switch. Simple and requires less cable than junction box method.
1.0mm² T&EMost common NI domestic wiring method
▲ the loop at rose method explained
The supply enters the first ceiling rose, loops out to the next rose, then the next. At each rose: live goes to the common terminal block, neutral connects, and a switch cable (both conductors become switched live) drops to the switch. The blue conductor in the switch cable must be sleeved brown to show it is a switched live, not a neutral. NI Building Control inspectors routinely check this.

Understand cable safe zones — NI rule
Where cables can go in walls without additional mechanical protection
SAFE ZONE ✓ — No extra protection needed
Where cables can run freely
  • Within 150mm of a wall-corner junction (vertical zone)
  • Within 150mm of ceiling or floor (horizontal zone)
  • Directly above or below an accessory (socket/switch)
  • In an earthed metal conduit anywhere in the wall
OUTSIDE SAFE ZONE ✗ — Protection required
Options when cable isn't in a safe zone
  • Install cable in earthed steel conduit in the chase
  • Use 30mA RCD protection for that circuit (most common in NI)
  • Use armoured cable (SWA) — very rarely done internally
  • NI practice: All-RCBO board means all circuits are RCD-protected — simplifies routing
▲ NI Building Control note on cable routing
NI Building Control inspectors check cable routing evidence during inspection. The most practical approach on a full rewire with an all-RCBO board is to route all cables in vertical chases from the accessory straight up to ceiling level, then run horizontally through the ceiling void or along the top of the wall. This satisfies safe zone requirements for the vertical drop and the horizontal run is hidden. Document cable routes on the floor plan — the EIC must note any departures from standard safe zone routing.
8
Chasing walls — method and depth
Cutting the channels in masonry or plasterboard for cables to run in
MASONRY WALLS (block/brick)
Using an angle grinder or SDS chisel
  • Mark two parallel lines with a straight edge — 25–30mm wide, 25mm deep
  • Score both lines with angle grinder (diamond blade) — wear full PPE: goggles, mask, gloves
  • Knock out the channel with a cold chisel and club hammer
  • Chase runs vertically to the accessory — never diagonal (dangerous & against regs)
  • Horizontal chases at ceiling or floor level only — no horizontal chases mid-wall
STUD WALLS (plasterboard/timber)
Drilling through noggins and threading
  • Locate studs with a detector — cables run between studs, not through them where possible
  • Where cables must cross studs, drill a 25mm hole through the centre of the timber
  • Never notch a stud — weakens it structurally
  • Thread cable using fish rods or stiff wire
  • Surface-mount in mini-trunking on exposed stud walls (garage etc.)
NI regulation — no diagonal chases: BS 7671 Reg 522.6.6 requires that cables in walls run vertically or horizontally only — never diagonally. A diagonal chase is harder to remember the route of and makes it easy for a future tradesman (plumber, joiner) to drill through the cable. Building Control inspectors look for this — diagonal cables will cause a fail.
9
Drilling floor joists — the correct method
Running cables under floors and through ceiling voids without weakening the structure
JOIST DRILLING RULES — BS 8103
Safe hole positions
  • Drill holes in the neutral zone: between 0.25D and 0.4D from the top of the joist
  • Maximum hole diameter: 0.25 × joist depth (e.g. 47mm max on 190mm joist)
  • Holes must be in the middle third of the span — not within 100mm of bearing points
  • Minimum distance between holes: 3× hole diameter
  • Use a flat wood bit or auger bit — not a spade bit (tears fibres)
PRACTICAL METHOD
Drilling and threading cable
  • Mark all joist centre-lines on the floor before drilling
  • Drill at same height through all joists in a run — stretch a string line as guide
  • Use fish rods or a stiff wire to pull cable through
  • Cable must not be tight — leave 10% slack in long runs
  • Where cable runs parallel to joists, clip to side of joist every 400mm

Running and routing all cables — room by room
The systematic order to run cables efficiently without missing anything
▲ Recommended running order — NI practice
Work from the consumer unit outwards, room by room. Label every cable at both ends as you go — use numbered cable ties or a permanent marker on masking tape wrapped around the cable. A cable you cannot identify at the CU end is useless and dangerous. Use the circuit schedule you designed in Step 2 as your checklist — tick off each cable run as it's completed.
LIGHTING CIRCUIT — running order
1.0mm² T&E from CU
  • Run cable from CU to first ceiling rose position (usually hall or landing)
  • Loop from rose 1 to rose 2 to rose 3 — always at ceiling level
  • Drop a switch cable from each rose to its switch (1.0mm² T&E or 3-core)
  • Leave 300mm tail at each rose, 200mm at each switch
  • Mark: "L1 — living room light" etc. at CU end and at rose
RING FINAL — running order
2.5mm² T&E ring out and back
  • Run from CU, up to ceiling level, then drop to each socket in turn
  • At each socket backbox: pass cable through, leave 200mm tail, continue to next socket
  • After last socket, return the ring back to the CU — separate cable, same route
  • At CU: two cable ends per circuit — label both "Ring L1 OUT" and "Ring L1 RETURN"
  • Check the ring returns to the same MCB slot as it left
SHOWER CIRCUIT
10mm² T&E — run this first (heaviest)
  • Run 10mm² T&E from CU to ceiling pull-cord isolator position
  • Then from pull cord down to the shower unit connection point
  • Keep the run as short and direct as possible — voltage drop matters on heavy cable
  • Support every 400mm on horizontal runs, every 500mm vertical
  • Leave 250mm at pull cord, 300mm at CU
COOKER CIRCUIT
6mm² T&E to CCU position
  • Run 6mm² T&E from CU directly to the CCU position on the kitchen wall
  • CCU position: 300–600mm horizontally from where the cooker will stand
  • Never route directly above the cooker position
  • Leave 250mm tail at CCU, 300mm at CU
  • Mark clearly — 6mm² looks like 2.5mm² when cut — label at both ends

Electrical work falls under Technical Booklet P (electrical safety) and Technical Booklet E (fire — smoke alarms). Unlike England/Wales, NI has no competent person self-certification scheme. All notifiable work requires District Council Building Control approval.

LAST SOCKET ON RING
Only one cable — not two
At the final socket before the cable returns to the CU, there will be only one cable in the box — the outgoing ring. The return cable goes directly back to the CU from this point, so only one set of conductors is in the last backbox. Wire it exactly the same but single cable only — L, N, E each get one conductor. SPURS FROM RING
Adding a non-fused spur
A spur is a branch off the ring to one additional socket. At the socket where the spur originates, there will be three cables: ring in, ring out, and the spur. Use WAGO connectors in the backbox to join all three lives together, all three neutrals, and all three earths, then run a single tail to each terminal of the socket. Assembly sequence for every socket: (1) Pull cable tails out of backbox. (2) Strip outer sheath 50mm. (3) Sleeve earth green/yellow. (4) Strip 8mm insulation. (7) Fold cables neatly into backbox — don't crush. (8) Screw faceplate — both screws, don't overtighten (cracks faceplate). (9) Test with voltage indicator when circuit is restored.

Wiring switches — 1-way and 2-way
Switch wiring involves switched live conductors — every conductor in the switch cable is a live, not a neutral
1-WAY SWITCH — 2 conductors used
COM and L1 only
  • Cable entering the switch box is the switch cable from the ceiling rose — 1.0mm² T&E
  • Brown conductor → COM terminal (permanent live supply from rose)
  • Blue conductor → L1 terminal (switched live back to rose) — must be sleeved brown
  • Earth → E terminal on switch (even if plastic switch — switch has an earth terminal)
  • L2 terminal is not used on a 1-way switch — leave empty or fit a blanking plug
2-WAY SWITCH — 3 conductors used
COM, L1, and L2
  • Use 3-core & earth cable between the two switches — brown, grey, black cores plus earth
  • At Switch A: COM = permanent live from rose. L1 = traveller 1. L2 = traveller 2
  • At Switch B: COM = switched live to lamp. L1 = traveller 1. L2 = traveller 2
  • Sleeve grey and black cores brown at both switches — all three are switched live conductors
  • This is the most commonly failed NI inspection point — don't skip the sleeving
Most common second-fix wiring fault — NI: The blue conductor in the switch cable is left un-sleeved (no brown sleeve). NI Building Control inspectors check every switch for correct sleeving on inspection. An un-sleeved blue in a switch cable will cause a fail. Buy a roll of brown sleeving before second fix starts and use it on every switch cable blue and every switch cable grey/black conductor.

The mandatory test sequence — follow this order
BS 7671 Part 6 specifies that tests are performed in this exact order — dead tests before live
Why the order matters: Dead tests must be done before live tests — you cannot safely perform an insulation resistance test on a live circuit (it will damage the tester and potentially cause electrocution). Polarity must be verified before Zs because a reversed polarity will give false loop impedance readings. Always follow the sequence exactly — it's specified in BS 7671 Section 634 for a reason.

Test 1 & 2 — Visual inspection and continuity

TEST 1 — VISUAL INSPECTION
Check before you measure

  • All switch cable blues sleeved brown

  • All faceplates secure and flush — no cracked or loose accessories
  • Correct cable types for each circuit location

  • Record pass/fail for each item on the visual inspection section of the EIC
TEST 2 — CONTINUITY OF CPCs (R1+R2)
Earth fault loop path resistance
  • Measures the resistance of the circuit from the furthest point back to the earth bar (R1 = line conductor, R2 = CPC)
  • Wander lead method: Link L and E together at the CU. Measure resistance at the furthest socket/switch from L terminal to E terminal — this gives R1+R2 in one measurement
  • Record R1+R2 for every circuit on the EIC schedule
  • Ring final test: Also measure R1 alone (both ring ends joined) — value should be consistent at every socket (within ±0.05Ω). An inconsistent reading indicates an open ring or crossed connection
  • Typical R1+R2 values: 2.5mm² circuit over 20m ≈ 0.4–0.8Ω

Test 4 — Polarity
Confirm every live terminal is actually live — and that switches break the live, not the neutral
WHAT POLARITY CHECKS
Done with supply OFF (continuity method)
  • Confirms that the brown conductor reaches the L terminal at every accessory
  • Confirms that switches break the live — not the neutral
  • Method: link L and E together at CU. At each socket, probe L terminal — should read low resistance back to E (confirming L is the brown conductor). If blue conductor is at L terminal: polarity reversed = FAIL
  • At each switch: probe COM terminal — should be L (live in). L1/L2 terminals are switched lives
  • Check every single accessory — polarity errors at any one point is a FAIL
QUICK CHECK — SOCKET TESTER
After circuits are live (supplementary)
  • A plug-in socket tester (£8–15) instantly shows correct wiring, live/neutral reversed, earth missing, or live/earth reversed
  • Use one at every socket as a quick functional check after live tests
  • It doesn't replace proper polarity testing — but it catches obvious faults fast
  • Common fault found: kitchen sockets with live and neutral transposed (wired to wrong terminals in a rush)
  • NI Building Control may ask to demonstrate a socket tester at inspection

Tests 5 & 6 — Earth loop impedance (Zs) and RCD testing
Live tests — supply on, test each circuit against BS 7671 maximum Zs values
HOW TO MEASURE Zs
Supply ON — use MFT in Zs mode
  • Set MFT to earth fault loop impedance (Zs) mode — typically a 2-wire measurement
  • At sockets: insert tester probes into L and E pins of the socket
  • At light switches: use test probe on L terminal and E terminal with switch closed
  • Test at the furthest point on each circuit — this gives the highest (worst case) Zs
  • Record the actual measured value — not just pass/fail
  • Compare to maximum in BS 7671 Table 41.3 for the MCB type and rating
  • If Zs exceeds maximum: check CPC continuity, check connections, check Ze
RCD TRIP TIME TEST
Two tests per RCD — NI mandatory
  • Test 1: Apply IΔn (rated trip current = 30mA) — must trip in under 300ms
  • Test 2: Apply 5×IΔn (150mA) — must trip in under 40ms
  • Also test at ½×IΔn (15mA) — must not trip (nuisance trip threshold)
  • Test from both a socket on that circuit and from the CU test button
  • Record all three trip times in the EIC test schedule
  • Typical good RCBO: 30mA test ≈ 15–25ms · 5× test ≈ 8–12ms
If Zs is too high on a circuit: Check R1+R2 for that circuit — a high Zs usually means a poor connection somewhere in the CPC path. Re-check every earth termination from the furthest point back to the CU. Common causes: loose earth in socket terminal, un-sleeved earth not making contact, incorrect cable size used for the CPC. On very long runs, an RCBO (which provides 30mA RCD backup protection) can compensate for a Zs that slightly exceeds the limit for a standard MCB.

The circuit path
Current flows: CU (6A RCBO) → Live wire (brown) → Switch COM terminal → L1 terminal → Switched live wire (blue sleeved brown) → Lamp L terminal → Lamp → Neutral (blue) → back to CU. When the switch is open, the path is broken and no current flows — the lamp stays off. The neutral wire runs directly from CU to lamp without going through the switch.
Why only the live is switched
BS 7671 requires that the live conductor is the one that's switched, never the neutral. If you switched the neutral, the lamp would be off but its internal components would still be live at mains potential — dangerous for anyone changing a bulb. Switching the live makes the lamp completely dead when off.

The traveller principle
Each switch has COM, L1, and L2 terminals. The live enters Switch A's COM. Two traveller wires run between the switches (L1 to L1, L2 to L2). The lamp only lights when both switches select the same traveller — forming a complete path. Toggle either switch and it breaks or completes the circuit regardless of the other switch's position.
The neutral path
The neutral runs directly from the CU to the lamp — it never goes through either switch. Only the live path is switched. This is why both conductors in the traveller cable are technically live at all times and must be sleeved brown at both switches.
NI critical: The traveller cable contains 3 cores plus earth. All three cores (brown, grey, black) are switched lives — none is a neutral. Grey and black cores must be sleeved brown at both Switch A and Switch B. NI Building Control specifically check this. Failure to sleeve is the single most common inspection fail on lighting circuits.

The intermediate switch
The intermediate (4-terminal) switch sits between two standard 2-way switches. In its straight-through position, it passes L1 to L1 and L2 to L2. When crossed, it swaps them — sending L1 out on L2 and vice versa. This is equivalent to toggling either end switch, so the lamp changes state regardless of what all three switches are doing.
Scaling up
You can add as many intermediate switches as needed — each sits between the previous one and the next. A corridor with 5 doors needs Switch A (2-way) + 3× intermediate + Switch B (2-way). Each additional intermediate requires a run of 3-core & earth between it and its neighbours. The end switches always remain standard 2-way types.

Grey and black cores must have brown sleeves fitted at every switch terminal. Ring continuity test method
Step 1: Join the ring-out and ring-return live conductors together at the CU (twist and tape). Step 2: Measure resistance at the L terminal to N terminal at each socket in turn — readings should be consistent and equal (±0.05Ω). Mid-ring sockets should read approximately half of end-to-end. Step 3: An unusually high reading at one socket indicates an open ring or crossed connection at that point. Step 4: Repeat for neutral conductors. Record all values on EIC test schedule.

Radial Circuit

Cable runs from CU to each socket in series and terminates at the last one — no return cable. Lower capacity than a ring but simpler. Used for kitchen appliances, garage, and small areas.

2.5mm² T&E (20A) or 4mm² T&E (32A) 20A or 32A RCBO Kitchen radial: AFDD mandatory recommendation
Radial vs Ring
  • Ring: cable goes out AND returns → 32A protection, 100m² area, halved resistance
  • Radial 20A: 2.5mm² T&E, terminates at last socket, 50m² max area
  • Radial 32A: 4mm² T&E, terminates at last socket, 75m² max area
  • Radial uses less cable but has higher resistance at far sockets — more volt drop
NI kitchen radial
NI Building Control increasingly expect kitchen sockets on a dedicated 20A radial with AFDD+RCBO (Reg 421.1.7). This separates kitchen appliances (high risk of arc faults from damaged leads) from the main ring final. Worktop sockets and under-counter appliances on the radial; fridge on a separate unswitched socket.

Wiring a Fridge / Freezer

A fridge plugs into the ring final — but needs its own unswitched socket. The compressor motor can occasionally trip RCDs, so individual RCBO protection for the kitchen circuit is important.

2.5mm² T&E (ring final) 32A RCBO on ring Unswitched socket — prevents accidental isolation Never on extension lead or adaptor
Why unswitched?
A fridge/freezer must never be accidentally switched off. An unswitched socket has no rocker switch — the only way to disconnect the appliance is to pull the plug. This prevents a family member flicking the socket off and causing food spoilage or worse. Always use an unswitched socket for fridges and freezers in NI kitchens.
RCBO vs RCD — compressor surge
Fridge compressors create a current surge on start-up that can occasionally trip a sensitive 30mA RCD covering many circuits. Using an individual RCBO per circuit means only the kitchen ring trips — not the whole house. This is one of the strongest arguments for all-RCBO boards in NI homes with modern appliances.
Never use an extension lead or adaptor with a fridge or freezer. Extension lead connections can arc or overheat when carrying continuous compressor load. Always connect directly to the ring final socket. The appliance must have its own dedicated socket — not shared with another high-draw appliance.
