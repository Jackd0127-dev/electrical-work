House Rewires Northern Ireland | What to Expect

House rewires in Northern Ireland

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

A house rewire is major electrical work. It normally involves design, circuit planning, safe isolation, removal or replacement of existing wiring, first fix, consumer unit work, second fix, inspection, testing and certification.

This page explains what a customer can expect during a rewire. It is not a DIY guide and must not be used as installation instructions.

When a rewire may be needed

A rewire may be recommended where an installation is old, damaged, unsafe, repeatedly faulty, unsuitable for modern loads, lacking adequate earthing or bonding, affected by alterations, or found unsatisfactory after inspection and testing. The need for a full or partial rewire should be confirmed by a competent electrician after assessment.

What customers should receive

After electrical installation work, customers should receive the appropriate certificate or report for the work carried out. The exact document depends on the type of work. Examples include an Electrical Installation Certificate, a Minor Electrical Installation Works Certificate, or an Electrical Installation Condition Report. The person responsible for the work should explain what document applies.

What the electrician will assess

  • existing wiring condition
  • consumer unit and protective devices
  • earthing and bonding
  • socket, lighting and fixed equipment needs
  • bathroom, kitchen and outdoor requirements
  • smoke, heat and CO alarm requirements where applicable
  • EV charger or future load requirements where applicable
  • cable routes and disruption
  • inspection, testing and certification/reporting requirements
Important

Do not attempt a rewire yourself unless you are competent to design, install, inspect and test electrical installations and issue the correct documentation. Unsafe electrical work can cause electric shock, fire, injury, death and serious property damage.

Consumer Unit Upgrades Northern Ireland

Consumer unit upgrades

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

A consumer unit upgrade is not just swapping a box. It requires assessment of the existing installation, supply characteristics, earthing and bonding, circuit condition, prospective fault current, maximum demand, protective device selection, RCD/RCBO requirements, SPD requirements, labelling, inspection, testing and certification.

Modern domestic consumer units commonly use non-combustible enclosures, clearly labelled circuits, residual current protection and surge protection where required by the design. RCBOs are often used to reduce inconvenience from faults, but the final arrangement must be selected by the competent person responsible for the design and verification.

Consumer unit work should be carried out by a competent electrician. Customers should expect pre-work checks, inspection and testing, clear circuit labelling, and the appropriate certificate or report after completion.

Important

Do not remove a consumer unit cover or work inside a consumer unit unless competent and authorised.

Manual confirmation required

Consumer unit wording must be checked by a qualified electrician before publication. This section intentionally avoids terminal, busbar, meter-tail and device-connection instructions.

Landlord EICR Northern Ireland | Electrical Safety Checks

Landlord electrical safety checks in Northern Ireland

Private landlords in Northern Ireland must have the fixed electrical installation in rented properties inspected and tested by a qualified person at least every 5 years, or sooner if the report requires it.

The inspection covers fixed electrical parts such as wiring, socket outlets, light fittings, consumer units and permanently connected equipment such as showers and extractor fans. It does not normally cover tenants' own plug-in appliances.

Landlords must obtain an electrical safety report, give the report to the tenant, give it to a new tenant before they occupy the property, provide it to a prospective tenant when required, provide it to the council if requested, and keep a copy for the next inspection.

If the report requires further investigation or remedial work, the landlord must arrange the required work within the stated period. A qualified person should carry out the work and written confirmation should be provided where required.

1 April 2025

The NI private tenancy electrical safety requirements apply to new private tenancies granted on or after this date.

1 December 2025

Existing tenancies granted before 1 April 2025 must comply by this date.

Qualified person wording

A qualified person should be competent to carry out the inspection, testing and any further investigative or remedial work in accordance with electrical safety standards. Landlords should check competence, suitable qualifications, insurance, inspection/testing experience and, where applicable, registration with a recognised electrical trade body.

Inspection and Testing Northern Ireland

Inspection, testing and electrical certificates

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

Electrical inspection and testing is a specialist task. New circuits, consumer unit changes, rewires and many alterations require suitable inspection and testing using appropriate calibrated equipment.

Depending on the work, inspection and testing may include visual inspection, continuity, insulation resistance, polarity, earth fault loop impedance, prospective fault current, RCD/RCBO checks and functional checks. The competent person carrying out the work should understand the installation, the current edition of BS 7671, and the correct documentation required.

Electrical Installation Certificate

Normally used for new installations, rewires, new circuits and significant installation work.

Minor Electrical Installation Works Certificate

Normally used for certain small additions or alterations that do not include a new circuit.

Electrical Installation Condition Report

Used to report on the condition of an existing installation after inspection and testing.

Important

The test button on an RCD or RCBO is a user functional check only. It is not a substitute for formal inspection and testing by a competent person.

Unsafe shortcuts are not acceptable

Any unsatisfactory test result must be assessed and corrected by the competent person responsible for the work. Protective devices must not be used as a casual workaround for poor design, poor earthing, damaged wiring or inadequate verification.

EV Charger Installation Northern Ireland

EV charger installations in Northern Ireland

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

Home EV charge points should be installed by suitably qualified specialist installers. EV chargers introduce high continuous loads and require proper assessment of the supply, maximum demand, earthing arrangement, open-PEN/PME protection, cable route, protective devices, RCD requirements, manufacturer instructions and network requirements.

In Northern Ireland, NIE Networks must be notified of an EV charge point installation. The installer normally provides capacity and testing information as part of this process.

Do not assume that an earth rod is always required, always prohibited, or always sufficient. The correct earthing/open-PEN solution depends on the charge point, supply arrangement, manufacturer instructions, current BS 7671 requirements and NIE Networks requirements.

Smoke, Heat and CO Alarms Northern Ireland Private Rentals

Smoke, heat and carbon monoxide alarms in Northern Ireland private rented properties

Private landlords in Northern Ireland must install and keep in proper working order sufficient smoke, heat and carbon monoxide alarms in rented properties. The landlord remains responsible for compliance even if an agent manages the property.

1 September 2024

The private tenancy alarm requirements apply to new private tenancies granted on or after this date.

1 December 2024

Existing tenancies granted before 1 September 2024 had to comply by this date.

Minimum alarms

  • one smoke alarm in the room most frequently used for general daytime living, normally the living room or lounge
  • one smoke alarm in every circulation space, such as halls, stairs, landings or corridors
  • one heat alarm in every kitchen
  • one carbon monoxide alarm in any room or circulation space containing a flue, including a chimney of an open fire not put beyond use, and/or a fixed combustion appliance other than a gas cooker or gas oven

Smoke and heat alarms must be ceiling mounted and interlinked. They must be installed and maintained in accordance with the relevant British Standard. Carbon monoxide alarms can be ceiling or wall mounted according to the manufacturer's instructions and relevant guidance.

If smoke, heat or carbon monoxide alarms are hardwired into the main electrical installation, the work must be carried out by a qualified electrician.

If mains wired alarms were installed to comply with Building Control requirements, for example when the property was built or extended, they must not be disabled. If they require replacement, they should be replaced in a way that maintains compliance.

Lighting and socket guidance

Sockets and lighting

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

Socket and lighting alterations should be designed and tested by a competent person. The electrician will consider the existing circuit, load, protective device, RCD/RCBO protection, cable route, safe zones, earthing/CPC continuity, accessories, environmental conditions and inspection/testing requirements.

Lighting circuits can include permanent line, neutral, switched line and CPC conductors. Existing installations may use older cable colours, borrowed neutrals, junction boxes, two-plate or three-plate wiring methods, or previous alterations. Do not rely on colour alone. Identification and testing are required.

Diagram policy

All socket and lighting diagrams must be simplified concepts only, not terminal diagrams. This site does not publish conductor termination instructions.

Electrical joints

Electrical joints should normally remain accessible for inspection, testing and maintenance. Where a joint is intended to be maintenance-free, the complete jointing system, including connector and enclosure, must be rated and installed exactly to the manufacturer's instructions and suitable for the environment.

A connector on its own should not be described as a buried or maintenance-free joint.

Bathrooms, kitchens and high-load circuits

Bathroom electrical work and high-load circuits

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

Bathroom electrical work

Bathrooms and shower rooms are special locations because the risk of electric shock is higher. Electrical work in these areas must be designed and installed with suitable equipment, appropriate IP ratings, correct locations, RCD/RCBO protection, bonding considerations, safe isolation, inspection and testing.

Electric showers, extractor fans, towel rails, underfloor heating, lighting, shaver supplies and switches must be assessed against the current edition of BS 7671, including the requirements for bathrooms and shower rooms.

Do not use this website to decide where to fit bathroom electrical equipment. The final design and equipment selection must be confirmed by a competent electrician.

Kitchen and high-load circuits

Kitchens often contain high-load fixed equipment such as ovens, hobs, cookers, dishwashers, washing machines, dryers, boiling-water taps and extraction equipment. Electric showers and EV chargers can also place heavy loads on an installation.

Circuit design cannot be chosen from a simple table. The electrician must assess the appliance rating, manufacturer instructions, maximum demand, diversity where applicable, cable route, installation method, grouping, insulation, ambient temperature, voltage drop, protective device, fault protection and RCD/RCBO requirements.

Any example values on this website are general context only and must not be used as design or installation instructions.

Manual confirmation required

Bathroom zone wording and all high-load circuit wording must be checked by a qualified electrician before publication. No bathroom zone diagram is included.

Earthing and protective bonding

Earthing and protective bonding

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

Earthing and protective bonding are essential safety measures. They help reduce the risk of electric shock by providing paths for fault current and by reducing dangerous voltage differences between conductive parts.

The correct arrangement depends on the supply type, main earthing terminal, service pipework, extraneous-conductive-parts, protective devices, cable sizes, distributor requirements and current BS 7671 design requirements.

Main protective bonding is not a cosmetic upgrade. It should be assessed and installed by a competent electrician where required.

Important

Do not use this website to size earthing or bonding conductors. The correct size and arrangement must be confirmed by design.

Manual confirmation required

Earthing and bonding wording must be checked by a qualified electrician before publication.

Garage and Outbuilding Electrical Supplies Northern Ireland

Garage and outbuilding electrical supplies

Short safety reminder

Customer information only - not installation instructions. Electrical work should be carried out by a competent electrician.

Detached garages, sheds and outbuildings require proper electrical design. The electrician must assess the supply type, earthing arrangement, extraneous-conductive-parts, bonding requirements, cable route, buried or exposed cable protection, RCD/RCBO protection, environmental conditions, load requirements and future use.

TT earthing is often used for detached outbuildings, but it is not a blanket rule for every installation. Exporting PME may or may not be appropriate depending on the installation. The earthing and bonding arrangement must be selected by a competent electrician after assessment.

Outdoor work warning

Outdoor and buried electrical work is not suitable for DIY guidance. Cable routes, mechanical protection, depth, warning markers, glands, IP ratings, corrosion protection and testing must be handled by a competent person.

Manual confirmation required

Outbuilding earthing, bonding and outdoor supply wording must be reviewed before publication.

Cable routes and building fabric

Cable routes and building fabric

Cable routes must be planned carefully. Concealed cables, wall chases, floors, ceilings, joists, fire barriers, insulation, damp areas and hidden services all create safety and building risks.

Safe zones, mechanical protection, RCD protection, earthed metallic covering, accessibility, fire-stopping and structural restrictions must be assessed by a competent person. RCD protection does not make an unsuitable cable route automatically acceptable.

Do not chase walls, drill structural members, alter fire-stopping or run concealed cables based on website guidance. Existing buildings may contain asbestos, hidden services, fragile materials or structural elements that require separate assessment.

Northern Ireland electrical compliance

Northern Ireland electrical compliance

Northern Ireland does not use the same domestic electrical Part P system as England and Wales. Do not assume that England/Wales notification or self-certification rules apply in Northern Ireland.

For ordinary domestic electrical installation work, Northern Ireland currently has no direct equivalent statutory domestic electrical requirement like England/Wales Part P. However, electrical work still needs to be safe and should be designed, installed, inspected and tested by a competent person to the current edition of BS 7671.

Other legal or formal requirements may still apply depending on the property and the work, including:

  • private rented property electrical safety duties
  • workplace duties under Northern Ireland health and safety law
  • Building Regulations requirements where electrical work forms part of wider building work
  • manufacturer instructions
  • NIE Networks requirements
  • insurance or mortgage requirements
  • local council Building Control requirements for the wider project

If the work is part of a new build, extension, conversion, major renovation, HMO, commercial premises, rented property, EV charger installation, or other controlled project, confirm the requirements with a qualified electrician and the relevant authority before work starts.

Technical Booklet P correction

Do not describe NI Technical Booklet P as electrical safety guidance. Technical Booklet P in Northern Ireland relates to sanitary appliances, unvented hot water storage systems and reducing the risk of scalding.

BS 7671 wording

Electrical work should be designed, installed, inspected and tested to the current edition of BS 7671 applicable at the time of the work.

BS 7671 is updated over time. As of 2026, BS 7671:2018+A4:2026 has been published, with transition arrangements from previous amendments. Before publishing technical content or carrying out work, confirm the current edition and amendment using the latest IET/BSI information.

Commercial, industrial and workplace electrical systems

Commercial, industrial and workplace electrical systems may fall under workplace health and safety duties, including the Electricity at Work Regulations (Northern Ireland). HSENI guidance states that electrical systems should be selected, erected and tested in accordance with appropriate standards such as BS 7671 for installations up to 1000 V, and that only competent persons should carry out work on electrical systems. Isolation and testing should take place before work starts.
