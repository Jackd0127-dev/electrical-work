NI Electrical Revision Reference

Compact UK/Northern Ireland electrical revision notes for competent-person learning and recap.

This app is a revision/reference aid for UK and Northern Ireland electrical learning and recap. It is aimed at competent persons, apprentices, improvers and qualified electrical workers who need a compact way to revise principles, terminology, test purposes and compliance context.

It is not a substitute for BS 7671, employer procedures, manufacturer instructions, site risk assessments, supervision or formal training.

Do not use this app as a public DIY wiring guide. Electrical work can cause electric shock, burns, fire, injury or death if designed, installed, isolated, tested or documented incorrectly.

Competence first

Only carry out electrical work that you are competent, authorised and equipped to do. If unsure, stop and ask a supervisor, qualified electrician, designer or inspector.

Current standard

BS 7671 changes over time. Treat edition/amendment references as review points and confirm against the current standard before relying on them.

Revision, not instruction

These notes explain concepts and checks. They do not replace project-specific design, safe isolation, inspection, testing or certification.

BS 7671 concepts

BS 7671 current-edition note

BS 7671 is updated by amendments. As of 2026, BS 7671:2018+A4:2026 has been published. During the transition period, the previous edition remains valid until 15 October 2026. After transition, check the latest IET/BSI position before relying on edition wording.

Do not hard-code detailed design answers from memory. For design, installation, inspection and testing decisions, check the current standard, manufacturer instructions and project documentation.

Needs competent review

Edition/amendment wording must be checked against current IET/BSI information before publication and after future amendments.

Northern Ireland compliance

Northern Ireland compliance recap

Part P correction

Northern Ireland does not use the same domestic electrical Part P system as England and Wales. Do not assume England/Wales notification or self-certification rules apply in Northern Ireland.

Domestic electrical work

Electrical Safety First states that Northern Ireland currently has no statutory domestic electrical installation requirement equivalent to England/Wales Part P. That does not mean work can be unsafe or undocumented. Electrical work should still be designed, installed, inspected and tested by competent persons to the current edition of BS 7671 and any applicable project, insurance, manufacturer, tenancy, workplace or Building Control requirements.

Technical Booklet P correction

Do not describe Northern Ireland Technical Booklet P as electrical safety guidance. Technical Booklet P relates to sanitary appliances, unvented hot water storage systems and reducing the risk of scalding.

When Building Control may still matter

Electrical work can still be relevant to wider Building Regulations-controlled projects, such as new builds, extensions, conversions, major alterations, fire safety work or controlled building work. Confirm project-specific requirements with the relevant authority and competent persons.

Workplace context

For workplaces, HSENI guidance expects electrical systems and equipment to be selected, erected and tested to appropriate standards such as BS 7671 for installations up to 1000 V. HSENI also states that only competent persons should carry out work on electrical systems and that isolation and testing should take place before work.

Needs competent review

NI compliance content must be checked against current official sources before publication.

Core principles

Safe isolation

Safe isolation is the process used to prevent dangerous voltage being present at the point of work. It is a life-critical control, not a paperwork exercise.

What it proves

  • the correct circuit/equipment has been isolated
  • the isolation is secure
  • the point of work has been proved dead
  • the test equipment is known to be working

Core principles

  • identify the correct circuit/equipment
  • isolate all relevant live conductors/supplies
  • secure isolation against re-energisation
  • display warning/lock-off where appropriate
  • prove the voltage indicator before and after testing
  • test at the point of work

Common mistakes

  • switching off a light switch and assuming the circuit is dead
  • relying on labels without verification
  • relying on conductor colours
  • forgetting borrowed/shared neutrals
  • not locking off
  • testing the wrong point
Revision only

Follow employer procedure, GS38-compatible test equipment requirements, site rules and supervision. Consider stored energy, alternative supplies, borrowed neutrals, backfeeds, UPS, PV, generators and control circuits.

Core principles

Core electrical safety principles

Shock protection

Revision topics include basic protection, fault protection, additional protection, barriers, enclosures, insulation, automatic disconnection of supply and the role and limitations of RCD/RCBO protection.

Fire protection

Common fire risks include overload, short-circuit faults, loose connections, poor terminations, thermal insulation effects, arcing faults, unsuitable equipment/enclosures and environmental conditions.

Fault path concept

Understand line-to-CPC fault paths, the earth fault loop path, supply transformer return path, CPC continuity, earthing and protective bonding.

ADS recap

The protective device must operate within required disconnection conditions. Zs and protective-device characteristics matter, and verification is required.

Common mistake

Do not assume an RCD makes any poor circuit safe. RCD/RCBO protection has specific purposes and limitations.

Terminology glossary

BS 7671

UK standard for electrical installations, published with amendments and used as the core wiring regulations reference.

Skilled / competent person

A person with sufficient knowledge, training, experience and judgement for the task and risks involved.

Line conductor

Live conductor that can be at dangerous voltage with respect to neutral or earth.

Neutral conductor

Conductor connected to the supply neutral point and used as the return path in normal single-phase operation.

CPC / protective conductor

Circuit protective conductor forming part of the protective fault path.

MET

Main earthing terminal, where earthing, bonding and protective conductors connect as applicable.

Main protective bonding

Connection intended to reduce dangerous potential differences involving extraneous-conductive-parts.

Earthing conductor

Conductor connecting the main earthing terminal to the means of earthing.

Extraneous-conductive-part

Conductive part not forming part of the electrical installation that can introduce a potential, usually earth potential.

Exposed-conductive-part

Conductive part of equipment that can become live under fault conditions.

Basic protection

Protection against contact with live parts under normal conditions.

Fault protection

Protection against electric shock under single-fault conditions.

Additional protection

Supplementary protection measure used in specified situations, often involving RCD/RCBO protection.

ADS

Automatic disconnection of supply, where a protective device disconnects a fault within required conditions.

RCD / RCCB

Residual current device; detects imbalance between live conductors. An RCCB does not provide overcurrent protection by itself.

RCBO

Device combining overcurrent protection and residual current protection for a circuit.

MCB

Miniature circuit-breaker providing overcurrent protection within its rated characteristics.

SPD

Surge protective device intended to limit transient overvoltages.

AFDD

Arc fault detection device intended to detect certain arc fault conditions.

IP rating

Ingress protection rating describing protection against solid objects and water ingress.

Zs

Earth fault loop impedance at a point in the installation.

Ze

External earth fault loop impedance of the supply at the origin, as relevant to the arrangement.

R1+R2

Resistance of line conductor plus CPC for a circuit path, used in verification and fault path assessment.

r1, rn, r2

End-to-end resistance values for ring final line, neutral and CPC conductors.

Insulation resistance

Test concept checking insulation condition between conductors.

Polarity

Verification that conductors and devices are arranged so live conductors are correctly controlled and protected.

Prospective fault current

Current that could flow under fault conditions at a point, relevant to equipment breaking capacity.

Voltage drop

Reduction in voltage along conductors caused by circuit load and impedance.

Diversity

Assessment of likely simultaneous load, applied with suitable guidance and judgement.

Final circuit

Circuit supplying current-using equipment or socket-outlets directly.

Distribution circuit

Circuit supplying a distribution board or similar distribution point.

Radial circuit

Final circuit running from origin to load points without returning to the origin.

Ring final circuit

Final circuit where line, neutral and CPC conductors form a ring back to the origin.

EIC

Electrical Installation Certificate for new installation work within its scope.

MEIWC

Minor Electrical Installation Works Certificate for certain minor work within its scope.

EICR

Electrical Installation Condition Report for an existing installation.

C1 / C2 / C3 / FI / LIM

EICR observation classifications requiring competent judgement and current guidance.

PME / TN-S / TN-C-S / TT

Supply earthing concepts. Confirm the actual arrangement on site; do not assume from appearance.

Circuits

Circuit concepts

Lighting circuits

Lighting circuits may contain permanent line, switched line, neutral and CPC conductors depending on wiring method. Older installations, alterations and different wiring methods can make conductor identification unreliable by colour alone. Identify by testing and documentation, not assumption.

  • permanent line and switched line
  • neutral at switch vs neutral at light
  • CPC continuity
  • borrowed/shared neutrals risk
  • two-way/intermediate switching concept
  • smart controls and neutral requirement
  • SELV/driver considerations for LED lighting

Socket circuits

Socket-outlet circuits may be radial or ring final circuits. The circuit arrangement, protective device, cable route, loading, RCD/RCBO protection and test results must all be considered.

  • radial concept
  • ring final concept
  • unfused spur concept
  • fused connection unit concept
  • load distribution
  • continuity of line, neutral and CPC
  • ring final testing purpose
  • limitations of visual inspection alone

Dedicated / high-load circuits

High-load or fixed equipment circuits require design around the actual load, manufacturer instructions, cable route, installation method, grouping, thermal insulation, voltage drop, protective device, isolation, RCD/RCBO requirements and certification.

  • cooker, hob or oven
  • electric shower
  • immersion heater
  • EV charger
  • heat pump
  • outbuilding supply
  • workshop equipment
No terminal diagrams

This module avoids terminal wiring diagrams, circuit ratings and cable-size recipes. Treat it as concept revision only.

Cable and design

Cable and design concepts

Cable selection factors

  • design current and future load
  • protective device rating/type
  • installation method and cable type
  • ambient temperature and grouping
  • thermal insulation and enclosure conditions
  • conductor material and circuit length
  • voltage drop and fault protection
  • mechanical protection and environment
  • manufacturer instructions and test results

Current-carrying capacity concept

Cable current-carrying capacity is not one fixed number. It depends on how the cable is installed and how heat can escape. Grouping, insulation, ambient temperature and enclosure conditions can reduce usable capacity.

Voltage drop concept

Vd = (mV/A/m x Ib x length) / 1000

Where mV/A/m is taken from the relevant cable data/table, Ib is design current, and length is circuit length in metres.

Use this as a revision formula only. Confirm permitted limits and values against current BS 7671 and cable data.

Zs and disconnection

Zs is the earth fault loop impedance at a point in the installation. It affects whether sufficient fault current will flow to operate the protective device within the required time. Zs depends on external loop impedance, circuit conductor resistance, temperature, connections and the protective path.

Diversity

Diversity is an assessment of likely simultaneous load, not a guess to make a design fit. It must be applied using appropriate guidance, experience and the nature of the installation.

Grouping and thermal effects

Cables grouped together or surrounded by insulation may run hotter. This can affect current-carrying capacity and protective device selection.

Design-dependent warning

Do not use this app to select final cable sizes or protective devices. Use current BS 7671, manufacturer data, design calculations and competent judgement. Do not use RCD/RCBO protection as a casual excuse for poor Zs or poor circuit design.

Protection

Consumer units and protective devices

A consumer unit brings together isolation, circuit protection, residual current protection where used, surge protection where fitted, circuit identification and distribution to final circuits. It is not just an enclosure.

MCB

Overload and short-circuit protection with device-curve and current-rating concepts. Final choices depend on design and verification.

RCD / RCCB

Residual current principle: imbalance between line and neutral. Provides additional protection in specified contexts but not overload protection by itself. The test button is functional only, not formal verification.

RCBO

Combined overcurrent and residual current protection for one circuit. It can reduce loss of multiple circuits compared with shared RCD arrangements, but still requires design and testing.

SPD

Surge protection concept for transient overvoltages and equipment protection. Selection depends on risk, installation, current BS 7671 and equipment.

AFDD

Arc fault detection concept intended to detect certain arcing faults. Requirements/recommendations depend on current BS 7671, circuit type, premises and risk.

Selectivity / discrimination

Correct device operation nearest the fault, upstream/downstream coordination, nuisance tripping and whole-installation loss are key revision points.

Labels and schedules

Clear circuit identification, accurate schedules, test documentation and spare-way records support safe operation and future work.

Limitations

Protective devices do not remove the need for sound design, correct installation, inspection, testing and documentation.

Diagram boundary

Do not include internal consumer-unit wiring diagrams, meter-tail instructions or terminal instructions.

Earthing and bonding

Earthing, bonding and supply types

Why earthing matters

Earthing provides a reference to Earth and a path for fault current under fault conditions. It supports operation of protective devices and reduces electric shock risk when correctly designed.

Why bonding matters

Protective bonding reduces dangerous potential differences between exposed-conductive-parts and extraneous-conductive-parts. It is not the same thing as earthing.

CPC

The circuit protective conductor forms part of the fault path. Continuity matters and must be verified.

MET

The main earthing terminal is a connection point for the earthing conductor, CPCs and bonding conductors as applicable. Arrangement varies by installation.

Supply type Basic concept Revision notes Cautions
TN-S Separate supply neutral and earth paths from source. Distributor earth may be provided by cable sheath or separate conductor. Confirm actual supply; do not assume from appearance.
TN-C-S / PME Neutral and earth combined in part of supply, separated at installation. Common arrangement with open-PEN considerations. EV, outbuildings and special cases need care.
TT Installation uses local earth electrode for earthing. Often relies on RCD protection because earth fault loop impedance can be higher. Electrode resistance, RCDs, testing and maintenance matter.
PME caution

This table is conceptual. Confirm the actual earthing arrangement on site. PME/TN-C-S arrangements need extra care for EV charging, outbuildings, outdoor equipment and situations involving extraneous-conductive-parts. Avoid blanket always/never PME statements; assess the installation.

Needs competent review

Main protective bonding requirements depend on whether service pipework or structural parts are extraneous-conductive-parts, the supply arrangement and current BS 7671. Do not size or omit bonding from memory.

Inspection and testing

Inspection, testing and certification

Inspection and testing verifies that an installation or alteration is safe and meets required design and installation criteria. This section is for revision of what tests are for and what results relate to. It is not a substitute for Guidance Note 3, current BS 7671, calibrated instruments, supervision or competence.

Initial verification

Includes inspection before testing, dead tests before live tests where applicable, live tests only by competent persons using suitable procedures, and documentation.

Continuity of protective conductors

Verifies CPC/bonding continuity, supports fault path verification and relates to R1+R2 and bonding checks.

Ring final continuity

Verifies continuity of line, neutral and CPC around a ring. It can identify open conductors, cross connections, figure-of-eight errors and loose terminations.

Insulation resistance

Checks insulation condition between conductors. It can reveal damage, moisture or connected-load/equipment issues. Sensitive equipment may need consideration.

Polarity

Verifies switching/protective devices are in the line conductor, socket polarity and correct conductor arrangement.

Earth fault loop impedance

Relates to loop impedance, fault path and protective device operation. It is affected by supply, length, conductor resistance, temperature and connections.

Prospective fault current

Helps confirm protective devices/equipment have adequate breaking capacity at origin and distribution points.

RCD/RCBO checks

Confirms residual current device operation under test conditions. The user test button is not formal verification.

Functional checks

Confirms operation of switches, controls, interlocks, indicators, alarms and equipment.

Special locations/systems

Smoke, heat and carbon monoxide alarms

Alarm requirements depend on property type, tenancy status, Building Regulations context, manufacturer instructions and current guidance. Northern Ireland private rented properties have specific alarm duties.

NI private tenancy recap

Private tenancy alarm requirements apply to new private tenancies granted on or after 1 September 2024. Existing tenancies granted before that date had to comply by 1 December 2024.

  • smoke alarm in the room most frequently used for general daytime living, normally living room/lounge
  • smoke alarm in every circulation space such as halls, stairs, landings and corridors
  • heat alarm in every kitchen
  • carbon monoxide alarm in any room or circulation space containing a flue, including a chimney of an open fire not put beyond use, and/or a fixed combustion appliance other than a gas cooker or gas oven

Smoke and heat alarms must be interlinked in private rented properties. Hardwired alarms must be fitted by a qualified electrician.

Needs competent review

Check current Department for Communities guidance before relying on alarm requirements.

Northern Ireland compliance

NI private tenancy electrical safety checks

Private landlords in Northern Ireland must have fixed electrical installations inspected and tested by a qualified person at least every 5 years, or sooner if the report requires it.

Compliance dates

New private tenancies granted on or after 1 April 2025 must comply from that date. Existing tenancies granted before 1 April 2025 must comply by 1 December 2025.

Inspection scope

Fixed electrical parts include wiring, socket-outlets, light fittings, consumer units and permanently connected equipment such as showers and extractor fans. Tenant-owned plug-in appliances are not normally part of the fixed installation inspection.

Report duties

Landlords must obtain an electrical safety report, provide it to tenants/new tenants/prospective tenants/councils where required, keep a copy, and arrange required remedial work or further investigation within the required period.

Revision point

Private-tenancy rules are legal/current-state content. Treat dates, report duties and scope as review-required.

Needs competent review

Check current Department for Communities guidance before relying on dates or duties.
